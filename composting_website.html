composting with a bin, how to start?

  1. choose a bin, Purchase a composting bin or make one from a plastic container. Ensure it has a tight-fitting lid to keep bugs out. and if it doesnt have holes, make them with a hot knife about 6mm wide to ensure ventilation
  2. pick a location, Choose a spot that's accessible and gets some shade. This could be on a balcony, in the kitchen, or outdoors. Avoid placing it in direct sunlight to prevent overheating.
  3. Prepare the Bottom Layer,add coarse material: Begin by adding a 5–8 cm layer of coarse, dry materials like small branches, twigs, or shredded cardboard. This improves airflow at the bottom of your bin.

    • greens:These are moist materials like fruit and vegetable scraps, coffee grounds, and grass clippings.
    • browns:These are dry materials like shredded paper, dried leaves, or straw.
    tips:
  • Follow a 2:1 ratio of browns to greens for balanced composting.
  • Check moisture: Your compost should be as damp as a wrung-out sponge. If it feels too dry, lightly spray water. If it’s too wet, add more dry browns.
  • Turn the pile: Every 1-2 weeks, use a garden fork or a shovel to mix the compost, improving air circulation and speeding up decomposition.
  • Your compost should heat up to around 55-70°C during decomposition. If it feels too cool, add more green materials to raise the temperature.(i recoment using a thermometer and sticking it to the inner side of the composting bin)
  • Chop large food scraps into smaller pieces (around 5 cm or less) to help them break down faster.
  • After several months(3-6) months, the material at the bottom of the bin will become dark, crumbly, and have an earthy smell. This indicates it’s ready for use.
  • when ready Mix the compost into garden soil, add it to potted plants, or spread it on your lawn for nutrient-rich soil.

Vermicomposting: A Quick Start Guide

Vermicomposting is a method of composting where worms (typically red wigglers) break down organic waste into nutrient-rich compost. It’s an efficient and eco-friendly way to recycle food scraps and create rich soil for plants.

  1. Set Up Your Worm Bin Use a container with a lid. Drill small holes for airflow, and line the bottom with moist bedding (shredded paper, coconut coir, or cardboard).
  2. choose the Right Worms Red wigglers (Eisenia fetida) are ideal for vermicomposting. You can buy them online or from local suppliers.
  3. Add Your Worms Place the worms into the prepared bedding. They will burrow in and begin adjusting to their new home.
  4. Feed Your Worms Add food scraps like fruit and veggie peels, coffee grounds, and eggshells. Avoid adding meat, dairy, or oily foods, as they can attract pests or create odors.
  5. Maintain Moisture and Temperature Keep the bedding moist like a damp sponge. Maintain a temperature of 55-77°F (13-25°C). Place the bin in a cool, shady spot.
  6. Harvest the Compost After 3-6 months, you’ll notice dark, crumbly compost. Push the contents to one side, add fresh bedding to the empty side, and let the worms migrate. Once they've moved, you can collect the compost and use it for your plants!

Vermicomposting is simple, sustainable, and great for the environment!

composting pile, a quick start guide

A composting pile is an easy way to recycle organic waste and turn it into nutrient-rich soil. All you need is space, the right mix of materials, and a little patience. Follow these simple steps to get started!

  1. Pick a shady spot in your yard with good airflow. Make sure it's accessible for easy maintenance.
  2. Create a Base Layer Start with a layer of coarse materials like twigs or straw, about 10 cm thick. This helps with drainage and airflow.
  3. Add Browns and Greens Layer browns and greens. Aim for a 2:1 ratio—twice as much brown as green.
  4. Maintain Moisture Your pile should feel like a wrung-out sponge. If it’s too dry, spray it with water. If too wet, add more dry material.
  5. Turn the Pile Every 2-4 weeks, use a garden fork or shovel to mix the compost. This helps aerate it and speeds up decomposition.
  6. Harvest Compost After 4-6 months, the bottom of your pile should be dark, crumbly, and smell earthy. That’s your finished compost, ready to use in your garden!

    7. tips for a good composting pile!

    • pick a shady location to make the pile dry less quickly
    • balancing the pile with a 2:1 ratio of browns and green is crutial to a good compost
    • keep the pile moist but not too soggy
    • mix the pile every now and then to make the compost speed up the composting proccess
    • compost should be ready in 4-6 months, and don't worry if the pile is hot, it is the micro organisms making gold from trash :)
